# Proposal Writing Prompt

A great proposal doesn't describe what you'll do — it describes what the client will have when you're done. This prompt writes proposals that focus on outcomes, not activities.

---

## The WEDGE Prompt

```
You are a senior consultant helping me write a winning proposal for a potential client engagement.

CONTEXT:
I am [your name], a [consultant/freelancer] specializing in [your specialty]. I just completed a discovery call with [client name] at [company]. Here's what I learned:

PROBLEM/PAIN POINT: [Describe their specific problem in 2-3 sentences. Use their words if you remember them.]

DESIRED OUTCOME: [What they said success looks like — what will be different when this is solved?]

DECISION MAKER(S): [Who will approve this? Who else is involved?]

BUDGET SIGNALS: [Did they mention a budget? Constraints? Previous spend on similar problems?]

COMPETITION: [Are they talking to other vendors? Do they have an internal option?]

TIMELINE: [When do they want to start? Any hard deadlines?]

MY PROPOSED APPROACH:
- Phase 1: [What you'll do first]
- Phase 2: [What you'll do second]
- Phase 3: [Deliverables/outcomes]

INVESTMENT:
- [Option A]: [Brief description] — $[price] ([timeline])
- [Option B]: [Brief description] — $[price] ([timeline])

DELIVERABLE:
Write a complete consulting proposal with the following sections:

**PROPOSAL STRUCTURE:**

1. THE SITUATION (1 short paragraph)
   Demonstrate you understand their problem better than they've articulated it. Mirror their words back with added precision. This section should make them think "Yes, exactly."

2. THE COST OF INACTION (1 paragraph)
   What is already being lost by not solving this? Make it concrete — time, money, opportunity, competitive position.

3. OUR RECOMMENDED APPROACH (2-3 paragraphs + phases)
   Explain what you'll do and why in a way that builds confidence. Not a list of activities — a coherent story of how you solve the problem.

4. WHAT YOU WILL HAVE WHEN WE'RE DONE (bulleted outcomes)
   3-5 specific, tangible deliverables. Make them concrete enough that both parties know whether they were delivered.

5. INVESTMENT OPTIONS (table or clear formatting)
   Present 2-3 options if possible. Anchor the highest, make the middle feel like the right choice.

6. NEXT STEPS
   Specific. "Reply to this email" or "Sign the agreement at [link]" — not vague.

7. ABOUT [YOUR NAME]
   2-3 sentences. Credibility without bragging. Include one specific proof point.

CONSTRAINTS:
- Tone: Professional but human. Write like a trusted advisor, not a sales pitch
- Length: 400-600 words for the full proposal. Long enough to be substantive, short enough to be read
- No jargon unless it's their jargon
- No passive voice
- Every section should make the client feel understood, not sold to

CLIENT DETAILS:
- Company: [Company Name]
- Industry: [Industry]
- Company size: [Approximate size]
- Primary contact: [Name, Title]
```

---

## Customizing the Output

After the AI generates the proposal, review it against this checklist before sending:

**Outcome check:**
- [ ] Does every section focus on what THEY get, not what you do?
- [ ] Is the problem statement in their language?
- [ ] Are the deliverables specific enough to be measurable?

**Tone check:**
- [ ] Does it sound like you wrote it? (Rewrite any section that doesn't sound like you)
- [ ] Is there at least one place where you show you understand their industry specifically?
- [ ] Does the investment section feel like a fair exchange for the outcomes described?

**Logistics check:**
- [ ] Is the next step frictionless? (One click, not "let me know your thoughts")
- [ ] Is pricing presented in a way that anchors correctly?
- [ ] Did you include your contact information?

---

## Delivery Tips

**Format:** Send as a Qwilr or PandaDoc page, not a Word attachment. Clients who open a link are 3x more likely to sign than those who download a file.

**Follow-up:** If no response in 3 business days, use the cold email prompt to write a brief, non-desperate check-in.

**Pricing psychology:** If you offer only one price, they decide yes or no. If you offer three, they decide which one. Always offer options.
